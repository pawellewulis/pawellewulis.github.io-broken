The puzzle once baptized as the hardest logic puzzle ever was described in an article of George Boolos published by The Harvard Review of Philosophy in 1996 where Raymond Smullyan is mentioned as the original author in the very first sentence. Without further ado, let's unveil the formulation of this puzzle.

<div align="left">
<i> 
Three goddesses stand before you: the Goddess of Truth, who always speaks truthfully; the Goddess of Lies, who always speaks falsely; and the Goddess of Fate, who answers truthfully or falsely at random. You do not know which goddess is which.

You may ask three yes/no questions, each addressed to exactly one goddess, in order to determine their identities. The goddesses understand English, but reply in their own language, in which the words da and ja mean yes and no in some unknown order.
</i> 
</div>

#### Relevant Assumptions

The following two clarifications are mentioned by Boolos himself in his article:

1) This clarification is  Any answer from the Goddess of Fate may be considered one of the da/ja answers generated by a coin toss. Since we are expected to provide a strategy solving the problem with certainty, we always need to consider both possibilites. 

2) You don't have to ask exactly one question to each of the goddessess. As we see later this possibility is actually relevant! The puzzle is impossible to solve otherwise. (TODO!)

3) You can decide which goddess receives a question depending on the  answers previously heard.

One extra detail worth addressing upfront is that some solutions attempt to 'hack' the problem by posing paradoxical questions with no well-defined answer, for instance:

To rule this case out, let us adopt the convention that any such question makes a goddess wrathful and she responds with a randomly chosen answer. The same applies to questions that fall outside the scope of her vast knowledge.

#### Key Difficulties

This problem is an excellent example on how to decompose a bigger problem into a bunch of smaller and easier ones. 

We may quickly identify the core challenges appearing here:
- We need to deal with the lying goddess.
- Does Da stand for Yes or No?
- One goddess simply provides random answers, which introduces informational fog. In particular, even if were told that e.g. Da is Yes and the Goddess of Lies was somehow forced to speak the truth, it is still not obvious how to proceed.

For each of these points we can construct a toy example isolating it. Later, we simply bring all these little pieces into a solution that emerges naturally.


# Two Guardians

The first issue we resolve is how to avoid getting tricked by the Goddess of Lies.  It is best to start with a simpler puzzle that's possibly even more well known that the Smullyan's puzzle that we discuss. I particularly like the lore of two guardians, but many have heard it as the 'knights and knaves' problem. The problem is now as follows.

<div align="left">
<i> 
Two guardians stand before you: one of them always speaks the truth; the other one always lies. One of the guardians has a key that you need to pass further.
You may ask a single yes/no questions addressed to exactly one guardian, in order to determine which one possesses the key.
</i>
</div>
<br>

**SOLUTION:** Ask any guardian the following question: *If you were the other guardian, which one of you would you point to as the one holding the key?* Then simply choose the guardian **not** indicated by the one you asked. 


### Analysis

Let us ponder a bit on the dynamics guiding the solution and formalize our discussion a bit. It might be reasonable to start with analysing some obvious questions. Perhaps:

<div align="left">
<b>Question:</b>
<i>Are you the one speaking the truth? </i><br>
<b>T:</b> 
<i> Yes. </i><br>
<b>F:</b> 
<i> Yes. </i>
</div>

That doesn't look very helpful. Even if both of the guardians answered (and actually only the one chosen would) we receive no information. Let's try another approach: 

<div align="left">
<b>Question:</b>
<i>Is it true that 2+2=4?</i><br>
<b>T:</b> 
<i> Yes. </i><br>
<b>F:</b> 
<i> No. </i>
</div>

Great. Now, we now which one is the truthsayer, but we have no idea who has the key. It's a tiny step forward, though. How about:

<div align="left">
<b>Question:</b>
<i>Do you have the key?</i><br>
<b>T:</b> 
<i> No. </i><br>
<b>F:</b> 
<i> No. </i>
</div>

In this scenario we can conclude that the liar has the key, but we can't tell which guardian is which.

Let us introduce some notation. Assume that $x$ is a sentence and $v(x)$ is its logical value, so $v(x)=1$ if $x$ is True and $v(x)=0$ if $x$ is False. The general form of the question we ask is:

$$ \text{Is it true that $x$?} $$

E.g. in one of the examples provided we had $x = \text{"2+2=4"}$. Regardless of the question, we expect one of the guardians to say Yes and the other one to say No. However, we also saw that the question "Do you have the key?" gave us 2 x No (or 2 x Yes). That is because we actually asked two different questions to the guardians. The question
$$ \text{Do you have the key?} $$
depends on the receiver. If we'd like to fully match the introduced formalization, we should rather label the guardians as Guardian 1 and Guardian 2, and then, consider a question like
$$ x = \text{"Guardian 1 has the key."}$$
Now the logical value of this statement remains independent on the receiver.

Let's refer to the truthsaying guardian as $T$ and to the lying one as $F$.
The well-known solution of the two guardians puzzle revolves around the idea of flipping the answers of $T$, so that essentially we are left with two liars. The guardian $T$ is asked to lie, so he lies. The guardian $F$ is asked to answer like $T$, but is still a liar, so he (?) lies as well.

Let's describe this reasoning using our rudimentary formalism:

- If we ask $T$, then the simplest way to get the answer is to simply ask if $x$ is True.
- If we ask $F$, then to get the answer we ask if $\neg x$ is True. 

We can combine these two points into a single question. Define

$$ \varphi(x) := (x \, \wedge \, \text{You are $T$}) \, \lor 
    \, (\neg x \, \wedge \, \text{You are $F$}). $$

Now, we ask any guardian:

$$ \text{Is it true that $\varphi(x)$?}$$

There are four possible cases: the guardian we ask may be truthful or not, and $x$ may be True or False. The resulting answers provided by the guardians are summarized below. The ticks and crosses indicate whether the question $\varphi (x)$ itself evaluates to True ($\checkmark$) or False ($\times$).


$$
\begin{array}{c|c|c}
\text{Input} & x & \neg x \\
\hline
T & \text{Yes } _\checkmark & \text{No } _\times \\
F & \text{Yes } _\times & \text{No } _\checkmark \\
\end{array}
$$

Any Yes answer means that $v(x)=1$, regardless of the guardian that we ask and the underlying sentence $x$. We managed to fully neutralize the liar. Now, we only need to set $x$ as "Guardian 1 has has the key." or even simply "You have the key."

# Language of Gods

Another issue to resolve is that the goddesses from the Smullyan's puzzle don't simply answer Yes or No, but rather Da or Ja. 

Let's try to isolate this particular difficulty and try to solve a simpler problem of this kind first. 

<div align="left">
<i> 
Two aliens stand before you: both of them always speak the truth. The aliens understand English, but reply in their own language, in which the words da and ja mean yes and no in some unknown order. One of the guardians has a key that you need to pass further. You may ask a single yes–no questions addressed to exactly one alien, in order to determine which one possesses the key.
</i>
</div>
<br>

Notice that we can't possibly know the logical value of these two statements at the same time:
- Da stands for Yes.
- Alien 1 has the key. 

Each of the two statements may be True or False, giving four cases in total. Any question you ask admits only two possible answers, which we identify with $\{0,1\}$, where $0$ stands for False and $1$ for True. Thus, each question yields at most one bit of information: two answers can distinguish at most between two possibilities, while here we have four. 

That means that we need to learn which alien is in possession of the key without ever learning the meaning of Da or Ja. Actually, all we need to do is to follow the route already described in the previous subsection. Define

$$ \psi(x) := (x \, \wedge \, \text{Da is Yes}) \, \lor 
    \, (\neg x \, \wedge \, \text{Da is No}). $$

Now, we ask:

$$ \text{Is it true that $\psi(x)$?}$$


$$
\begin{array}{c|c|c}
\text{Input} & x & \neg x \\
\hline
\text{Da is Yes} & \text{Da } _\checkmark & \text{Ja } _\times \\
\text{Da is No} & \text{Da } _\times & \text{Ja } _\checkmark \\
\end{array}
$$

Again, we reached a configuration where Da simply indicated that the underlying question $x$ is true. The rest follows easily by simply setting $x$ as 'You have the key.' again.

# Even Aliens Can Lie

Take the same puzzle about aliens from the previous section, but add an extra assumption that one of the aliens always lies. Just like with the guardians, we can label these aliens as $T$ and $F$, but we do not know which is which. Therefore, we've got $8$ possible cases now: an alien can lie or not, Da can mean Yes or No, and an alien has the key or not. It turns out that to get the answer to any question of our choice, we only need to combine $\varphi (x)$ and $\psi (x)$ described in the previous sections into a single monster-sentence:

$$ (\psi \, \circ \, \varphi ) (x) = 
    (\varphi (x) \, \wedge \, \text{Da is Yes}) \, \lor 
    \, (\neg \varphi (x) \, \wedge \, \text{Da is No}).  $$

This sentence can be expanded as:

$$ 
    (x \, \wedge \, \text{You are $T$} \, \wedge \, \text{Da is Yes}) 
    \, \lor \, 
    (\neg x \, \wedge \, \text{You are $F$} \, \wedge \, \text{Da is Yes}) $$

$$  \, \lor \,
    (x \, \wedge \, \text{You are $F$} \, \wedge \, 
    \text{Da is No})
    \, \lor \,
    (\neg x \, \wedge \, \text{You are $T$} \, \wedge \, \text{Da is No}) 
    . $$

The resulting table of possible answers looks like that:

$$
\begin{array}{c|c|c}
\text{Input} & x & \neg x \\
\hline
T \, \wedge \, \text{Da is Yes} & \text{Da } _\checkmark & \text{Ja } _\times \\
T \, \wedge \, \text{Da is No} & \text{Da } _\times & \text{Ja } _\checkmark \\
F \, \wedge \, \text{Da is Yes} & \text{Da } _\times & \text{Ja } _\checkmark \\
F \, \wedge \, \text{Da is No} & \text{Da } _\checkmark & \text{Ja } _\times \\
\end{array}
$$

As we can see, we hear Da back whenever $x$ is True and Ja the otherwise.

# Solution!

We've got three goddesses, denoted by $T, F, R$, where $R$ represents the Goddess of Fate. Let's label the goddessess in whatever order as $G_1$, $G_2$ and $G_3$. Our task is to determine which element of $\{T, F, R\}$ corresponds to each $G_i$ for $i=1,2,3$. Equivalently, we are looking for the correct permutation. For example, one possible assignment is:
$$ G_1 \mapsto F, \\ G_2 \mapsto T, \\ G_3 \mapsto R.$$

There are 6 such arrangements, and our goal is to identify the correct one.

Let's take advantage of our findings from the previous sections. We already know that if we want to know if $x$ is True, we may ask:

$$ \text{Is it true that $(\psi \circ \varphi)(x)$?}$$

This way we neutralize the difficulties arising from the fact that one goddess always lies and that we don't speak the language of gods. We effectively reduced the main puzzle into a simpler one:

<div align="left">
<i> 
Three goddesses stand before you, called T, F, and R. Both T and F always speak truthfully; R answers truthfully or falsely at random.

You may ask three yes/no questions, each addressed to exactly one goddess, in order to determine their identities.
</i> 
</div>

Let's quickly notice that if $R$ also spoke truthfully at all times, this puzzle would become trivial. We could split the set of $6$ possible permutations into two sets $S_1$ and $S_2$, each of the size of $3$ and simply ask:

$$ \text{Is it true that the correct permutation belongs to $S_1$?}$$

This way we are left with only $3$ options left. The next question would allow us to reduce the set of candidates to at most $2$, and with the third question we'd be done. 

Since any question provides us with exactly $1$ bit of information, then three questions are enough to describe at most $8$ possible states. In our problem one bit is 'foggy', because R can lie. It might initially seem that any answer given by R is worthless, but it turns out not to be the case. We ask $G_1$ the following question:

$$ \text{Is it true that $G_2$ is $R$?}$$

The answer actually tells us a lot:
- If the answer is Yes, then either it's true and $G_2 = R$ or it's a lie, which means that $G_1=R$. As a result we learn that certainly $G_3 \not= R$.
- Analogously, if the answer is No, then $G_2 \not= R$. 
- Now, we identified at least one goddess who always speaks the truth. From this point on, we can simply ask all the questions to her. 
- We are also left with only $4$ permutations left, because if for example $G_2 \not= R$, then both $(T, R, F)$ and $(F, R, T)$ are out.

We are now done, because two questions are exactly enough to identify which of the $4$ candidate permutations is the correct one!

# Is it 'a Solution' or 'the Solution'?

A proverbial careful reader might've noticed that the question I chose for $G_1$ in the previous section is a bit arbitrary. It does the job, but is it necessary?

First, let's notice that every question we could ask is meant to identify some subset of permutations of $\{T,F,R \}$ that can match the true arrangement. Essentially, any question is in this sense equivalent to asking something akin to:

$$ \text{Does the correct permutation belong to the set} \\
S := \{(T,F,R), (R,T,F), (F,R,T), (T,R,F)\} \text{?} $$

where $\mathcal{S}$ can be any subset of these 6 permutations we could think of. In particular, if $\mathcal{S}$ contained all the permutations, then the question above is equivallent to asking about any statement we know beforehand to be True. 


We've got exactly $3!=6$ possible permutations to consider, and so, $2^6 = 64$ possible questions (up to the equivalence relation).

What are the necessary conditions a question needs to satisfy to lead us to a solution? 
- It needs to leave us with at most 4 candidate permutations. Otherwise, two questions are not enough, even if we are sure that it is not $R$ who answers.
- We need to be able to idenfity one goddess who is not $R$.

Let's quickly explain why the second condition is indeed necessary.
